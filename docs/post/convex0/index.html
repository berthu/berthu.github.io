Common Techniques for Convex Optimization Formulations

Here are some techniques that might be useful in convex optimization.

Techniques relating to positive semi-definite matrices

Definition: A symmetric matrix $M$ with real entries is positive-definite if the real number $z^{\intercal} M z > 0$ for every nonzero column vector $z$. The matrix is positive semi-definite if the quantity is weakly positive.

Note: A similar object can be defined for complex matrices, but instead of a symmetric matrix, consider a Hermitian matrix, or a complex matrix that is equal to its own complex conjugate.

Conceptually, the definiteness of a matrix describes the shape of its quadratic form, such that a minimum exists. Importantly, a function $f$ is convex if and only if its Hessian is positive semi-definite.

Rank-one square matrices are positive semi-definite

Matrices that can be written in the form $z z^{\intercal}$ where $z \in \mathbb{R}^n$ must be positive semi-definite, since for any $x$, $x^{\intercal} z z^{\intercal}x = (z^{\intercal}x)^2 \ge 0$. It is called a rank-one matrix, since multiplying $z z^{\intercal}$ by any $x$ equals $z(z^{\intercal}x)$, which is just z, one-linearly independent column vector, multipled by a constant.

Corollary: Any finite sum of rank-one square matrices are positive semi-definite.

Example

Consider the function

$$f(x_1,x_2) = x_1^2/x_2$$ on $\mathbb{R}_{++}^2$. Its Hessian is:

$$ \nabla^2 f = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} $$
